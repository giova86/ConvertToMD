Introduction

The atomic nucleus is a complex many-body quantum system, displaying a rich variety of fascinating phenomena. In the past few decades, a large amount of collected data has been obtained for thousands of nuclei, from hydrogen to actinides, leading to a general understanding of their properties, especially in proximity of the valley of stability. However, there is no doubt that we have just started to scratch the surface of the entire nuclear landscape, both experimentally and theoretically.

From the experimental point of view, out of a total of more than 7000 nuclei, estimated to exist in the universe, about 3600 have been studied in laboratories, so far. In particular, the vast majority of the isotopic chains - through which the physics processes responsible for the formation of the heavy elements in the universe run (such as the r-process path [1, 2]) - are very little explored. The location of these processes on the chart of nuclei is also very uncertain and strongly dependent on the most basic features of the systems involved, as for example shapes and deformations [3].

From the theory point of view, our limited knowledge of the nuclear interaction and the difficulties in dealing with a many body problem, with up to more than 200 constituents, have prohibited to achieve a unified description of the structural properties through the entire nuclear landscape. In absence of an exact theory, various models have been developed, based on effective interactions derived from experimental observations

---

<!-- page 10 -->

in specific regions of masses. As a consequence, predictions are mainly limited to systems a few nucleons away from experimentally observed nuclei, and extrapolations to exotic regions, far away from the valley of stability, suffer from large uncertainties.

The objective of this thesis work is the experimental investigation of the coupling between vibrations (phonons) and single particle excitations in the atomic nucleus. This is recognized as on of the main building block entering the description of the interplay between individual nucleonic motion and collective degrees of freedom – a central issue in nuclear physics, directly related to the dual nature of the atomic nucleus: an aggregate of individual constituents which both retain their individual character but also act coherently, in a collective way. Excitations of single nucleons, on one hand, vibrations and rotations, at the other extreme, are clear manifestations of this duality.

Odd systems around doubly magic shell closure are an ideal playground for studying the dual nature of the nucleus: they give simultaneous access to single particle excitations and to multiplets of states arising by coupling the odd particle with excitation of the closed shell core. Collective vibrations, i.e. phonons, are the most important low-lying states of spherical nuclei, therefore particle-phonon coupled states are expected to dominate the low-lying spectrum of one-valence particle nuclei.

It is important to underline that correlations with surface vibrations, often referred to as long range correlations [4, 5], have been recognized, since long, as the main responsible for the fragmentation of the single particle strength [6–10], anharmonicity effects in vibrational spectra [4, 5, 11], damping phenomena in giant resonances [12] and, very recently, the reduction of β-decay half-lives in magic nuclei [13]. Moreover, the coupling between phonons and particles is at the basis of interacting fermionic many-body systems, both in nuclear physics and in condensed matter physics [14]. It is therefore clear that a systematic study of couplings phenomena around doubly magic (and semi-magic) shell closures, in different regions of the nuclear chart, both experimentally and theoretically, is very much needed in order to significantly advance our understanding of the effective nuclear force (for what concerns, in particular, correlation terms between nucleons), aiming to reach, on a longer term, a fully microscopic description of the emergence of complex excitations in the nuclear system.

Experimentally, a number of difficulties has hampered an extended spectroscopy of odd-systems, with respect to closed shell cores, particularly in the neutron rich region, not least their limited or even impossible population by standard stable beam reactions, such as fusion. From the theory point of view, a severe limitation in the description of particle-phonon coupled states comes from the impossibility in performing proper microscopic calculations of collective excitations, such as phonons, in medium-heavy masses, due to the divergent number of different configurations to be taken into account.

As a consequence, the interpretation of the experimental data has been based so far – almost entirely – on a phenomenological approach, originally developed by A. Bohr and B. R. Mottelson in the 70’s [4, 5].

In Chap. 1, the status of the experimental investigation in the Pb, Ca and Ni region will be briefly recalled, with special emphasis on the works performed prior to this thesis work in the framework of the research activity performed within the Nuclear Structure research group in Milano. This will help setting the stage for the experimental results discussed in this thesis (presented in Chaps. 4 and 5), concerning nuclei in the proximity of very different shell closures. First, the doubly magic shell closures of $^{40}$Ca and $^{48}$Ca, at the stability valley, are considered, followed by the investigation of the exotic region of $^{132}$Sn. In particular, systems made of one-valence particle only, such as $^{41}$Ca, $^{49}$Ca and $^{133}$Sb, will be experimentally investigated, taking advantage of $\gamma$ spectroscopy following reactions induced by cold neutrons, i.e., capture reactions and induced fission of actinides targets.

The experiments are part of the EXILL campaign, conducted in 2012-2013 at the reactor of Institut Laue-Langevin (ILL) in Grenoble, delivering the world’s brightest continuous neutron beam, for research studies. In that occasion, for the first time and for about 100 days, an efficient array based on up to 52 HPGe detectors, complemented by fast scintillators of LaBr$_3$:Ce type, has been placed in front of such a high intensity neutron source (of the order of $10^8$ ns$^{-1}$ cm$^{-2}$), allowing to perform high statistics $\gamma$ spectroscopy of fission products, around $^{132}$Sn and north-west of $^{78}$Ni, besides a number of neutron capture studies on rare targets. A key point of the setup was the fully digital, triggerless acquisition system (with time stamp intervals of 10 ns), which allowed to handle event rates up to 0.84 MHz and to study, for the first time, coincidences among $\gamma$ transitions separated in time by several tens of microseconds (see Chap. 2).

The unique combination of an intense neutron beam, a powerful array and innovative experimental techniques (described in Chap. 3), offered by EXILL, has allowed to push $\gamma$ spectroscopy at the limit of sensitivity: in the case of $^{133}$Sb, one of the main objectives of this thesis, positive yrast states have been extended up to spin $25/2^+$, above the very longed lived, 17 $\mu$s isomer. Moreover, the presence of fast scintillators allowed to measure lifetimes of selected states (i.e., the $13/2^+$ and $15/2^+$). The results pointed to a large difference (almost 2 orders of magnitude) between decay probabilities of M1 transitions depopulating consecutive yrast levels: this cannot be explained by standard shell model calculations, clearly suggesting a complex structure for these excitations.

As discussed in Chap. 5, the new data led to a significant step forward in theory: the development of a new self-consistent microscopic approach, called “Hybrid Configuration Mixing” model [15], taking into account, for the first time, excitations of the core

The model, presented in Chap. 6, has no free parameters and is self-consistent in the sense that both single-particle states and phonons come out of Hartree–Fock (HF) and Random Phase Approximation (RPA) calculations performed with the Skyrme SkX interaction [16]. CHAPTER 1

Physics Background

In this chapter, the physics motivation behind the work presented in this thesis will be discussed.

In the first part, the physics case associated to the study of nuclear excitations arising from the coupling between single-valence particles and collective excitations of the core nucleus is briefly recalled. The status of the research field, prior to this thesis work, will be presented from both an experimental and a theoretical perspective. Special emphasis will be given to recent results obtained around Ca, and Ni neutron rich magic and semi-magic cores, which are relevant for the subsequent discussions.

In the second part of this chapter, the main features of neutron capture and neutron induced fission reactions will be presented in a concise way, being the reaction mechanisms used for the production of the experimental data on which this research work is based.

1.1 Couplings between particles and phonons

One of the main goals of nuclear physics is the investigation of the correlations between collective excitations and single-particle motion. In particular, the coupling between the motion of the individual particles and vibrations of the nucleus is a central issue: this is in fact a key ingredient entering the description of much more complex phenomena,

---

<!-- page 14 -->

such as the anharmonicities of vibrational spectra, the damping of giant resonances and the quenching of spectroscopic factors [1–3]. In general, the coupling between phonons and particles is at the basis of interacting fermionic many-body systems, both in nuclear physics and in condensed matter physics [4].

In nuclear physics, magic or doubly magic nuclei (i.e., with one or both the proton/neutron shell closed) are characterized by low-lying collective excited states, such as quadrupole and octupole vibrations, which may be particularly intense, giving rise to $2^+$ and $3^-$ discrete excited states at excitation energies in the 2-4 MeV range, above the ground state. As a consequence, nuclei with one or few particles/holes outside a “core” (i.e., a closed shell nucleus) are the best systems to investigate both single particle excited states and states arising from the coupling between core excitations and single particles. A cartoon-like view of the particle-phonon coupling phenomenon is given in Fig. 1.1.

The coupling between a phonon, with multipolarity $\lambda$, and a particle of spin $j$, gives rise to a multiplet of states with angular momentum $J$ equal to

$$|\lambda - j| \leqslant J \leqslant |\lambda + j|$$

and values of the electromagnetic transition probabilities to the ground state which are related to the properties of the core and to the intensity of the coupling. In the case of odd-nuclei with only one particle outside the core, if the coupling is weak, one may expect that the reduced decay transition probabilities from the states of the multiplet to the odd nucleus ground state are dominated by the reduced transition probabilities of the

---

<!-- page 15 -->

core vibration.

Experimentally, a very precise and unique identification of the members of the multiple is, therefore, first of all needed: the energy of each level and, in particular, the spin and parity should be assigned by measuring, for example, the angular correlation/distribution and the polarization of the radiation emitted from the multiplet states (see Sec. 3.2). Secondly, information concerning the nature of the states is needed, and this is provided by the electromagnetic transition probabilities $B(E/M\lambda)$ values: they can be derived by the measurement of the lifetime $\tau$ of the state of interest, as discussed in Sec. 3.3.

From the discussion above, it follows that an appropriate theoretical interpretation of these complex excitations implies the capability of describing, simultaneously, collective as well as single particle excitations. This may not be a simple and possible task within a shell model approach, especially in medium-heavy systems, due to the large number of nuclear configurations to be taken into account [17]. As a consequence, alternative approaches must be used if collective excitations have to be considered.

Historically, the first approach to the particle-vibration coupling problem was introduced by Bohr and Mottelson in the 70’s: it aimed at describing, phenomenologically, the first experimental evidence of a septuplet of states observed in $^{209}$Bi, following deuteron inelastic scattering on a $^{209}$Bi target [4]. This is the model most extensively employed, so far, in the description of particle-phonon coupled states. However, it has a strong limitation: it is tailored to describe cases in which the excitation is dominated by the coupling with one specific phonon (particularly strong), using a perturbative approximation, as discussed in Sec. 1.1.1. In reality, in most of the cases a more complex scenario is realized: collective phonons are not the only mode of excitations of the core system and states having real phonon character coexist with excitations with less collective character, which should then be considered when treating the coupling with an odd particle. As discussed in Chap. 5, this consideration together with the need of describing the $^{133}$Sb data, one of the main objective of this thesis, have lead to the development of the “Hybrid Configuration Mixing” model. This is a microscopic and self-consistent approach which deals with both single particle states and states emerging from Random Phase Approximation (RPA) calculations$^1$ [22], but can have either collective character or pure p-h nature. In this respect, the model aims at making a significant, first step toward a

$^1$The Random Phase Approximation (RPA), introduced by David Bohm and David Pines between 1951 and 1953 to solve the many-electron problem for the uniform electron gas [18–20], is an effective theory developed in condensed matter physics and well known in nuclear physics field for computing nuclear excitations as linear combinations of one-particle/one-hole (1p-1h) and one-hole/one-particle (1h-1p) excitations. This kind of approach, principally used to describe excitations with high degree of collectivity, has been applied to study excitations on a wide energy range, from a few MeV (the discrete spectrum), up to hundreds of MeV. One the most important successes of the RPA is the prediction of collective surface vibrations, called Giant Resonances, appearing at energies between 15 and 30 MeV [12, 21].

In the following, the status of the experimental investigation of particle phonon coupled states is given, focusing in particular on four, relevant, mass regions: i) the $^{208}$Pb region, ii) the $^{48}$Ca region, iii) the semi-magic Ni isotopic chain, from $A = 60$, to $A = 68$, and iv) the mass region of the exotic $^{132}$Sn core, main objective of this thesis work.

1.1.1 The $^{208}$Pb region

The region of nuclei around $^{208}$Pb has been the subject of extended investigations, both experimentally and theoretically. This is related to the fact that $^{208}$Pb is a very good doubly magic nucleus, therefore the structure of neighbouring nuclei, having or lacking nucleons with respect to it, can be appropriately described in terms of shell model. A peculiarity of $^{208}$Pb is the presence of a very collective octupole excitation at very low

---

<!-- page 17 -->

excitation energy: the 3$^-$ state is located at 2.615 MeV above the ground state and has a B(E3) strength of 34 W.u., one of the largest of the entire nuclear chart [23, 24]. As a consequence, one-particle (1p) or one-hole (1h) nuclei around $^{208}$Pb show states originating from couplings of the 3$^-$ phonon with single particle/hole [25–27]. The most famous case is the one-proton nucleus $^{209}$Bi in which the entire multiplet arising from the coupling of a $h_{9/2}$ proton with the 3$^-$ phonon of $^{208}$Pb has been located [28]: it consists of a septuplet of states with spin and parity $I^\pi = 3/2^+, 5/2^+, 7/2^+, 9/2^+, 11/2^+, 13/2^+$ and $15/2^+$. Experimental investigations by (d,d') and (p,p') provided a complete spectroscopy of the septuplet, as shown in Fig. 1.3. Indeed, the observed small splitting of the multiplet components (i.e., 7 states within less than 300 keV) immediately suggested a weak coupling between the odd proton and the octupole vibration, as originally proposed by Bohr and Mottelson, within a phenomenological approach [5]. Besides this seminal work, the particle-vibration coupling phenomenon in the $^{208}$Pb region has been studied by several authors in the last fifty years. In particular, the works by I. Hamamoto [29] and P.F. Bortignon et al., [30] should be mentioned: the former provided accurate predictions for cross sections and for the sequence and the energies of

---

<!-- page 18 -->

the septuplet, while in the latter case a field theoretical approach to the nuclear structure was proposed and the results were compared with more traditional exact treatments, i.e. group theoretical and Feynman-Goldstone diagrammatic perturbation techniques [31, 32]. These studies laid the foundations for a general and systematic search for the particle-vibration coupling phenomena in different regions of the nuclear chart.

1.1.2 The $^{48}$Ca region

The historical results obtained in $^{208}$Pb region motivated the investigation of the coupling between particle and collective vibration also in other systems. Experimentally, several indications have been found of discrete states of particle–phonon nature especially in the medium-heavy nuclei [4, 5, 26, 33–38], however, a crucial point still to understand is, to what extent states of particle–phonon nature can be considered a general nuclear property, also in rather light systems with reduced collectivity.

In the lower mass region, Ca isotopes represent good benchmark systems: in $^{48}$Ca the $2^{+}$ state has very limited collectivity ($B(E2) = 1.9$ W.u.), while the $3^{-}$ has a rather strong collective character ($B(E3) = 6.8$ W.u.) [33, 34]. Therefore, it become interesting to verify if these properties manifested themselves also in adjacent nuclei, such as for example in the one valence neutron nucleus $^{49}$Ca.

A complete in-beam $\gamma$-spectroscopy study of $^{49}$Ca was performed by Montanari et al., in Refs. [39–41]. Making use of the multi-nucleon transfer reaction $^{48}$Ca (at 6 MeV/A) $+^{64}$Ni, at energy approximately twice the Coulomb barrier, states in $^{49}$Ca were observed up to 4 MeV of excitation energy, and their spin and parity was established by angular distribution and polarization measurements (see Fig. 1.4). Lifetime measurements provided evidence for two different types of particle–core couplings, either based on particle–phonon interactions or characterized by 2p-1h simple configurations. The results were compared with theoretical predictions based on the phenomenological weak-coupling model developed by A. Bohr and B. R. Mottelson in Ref. [5]. The comparison pointed out that the $9/2^{+}$ state at 4017 keV in $^{49}$Ca represents one of the few fully established examples of particle–vibration coupling in nuclei with mass $A < 100$, showing the robustness of nuclear collectivity in rather light systems.

In Chap. 4 of this thesis, additional data on $^{49}$Ca, obtained following cold neutron capture during the EXILL campaign, will be presented and compared with the newly developed “Hybrid Configuration Mixing model”. This new approach will be found to provide a satisfactory reproductions of complex excitations also in this rather light mass region.

1.1.4 The $^{132}$Sn region

In the framework of the investigation of both shell model-type excitations and coupling phenomena between single particles and core excited states, the region around $^{132}$Sn is particularly attractive, since $^{132}$Sn is a doubly magic nucleus in an exotic region of the nuclear chart.

From the theory point of view, the $^{132}$Sn region has always been the subject of extended investigations:

i) it bridges the gap between the lighter doubly closed-shell systems ($^{16}$O, $^{40}$Ca, $^{48}$Ca) and $^{208}$Pb, therefore it plays an essential role in developing models with universal nucleon–nucleon interactions [57, 58];

ii) it plays a crucial role in answering the question whether the traditional shell-gap properties of $Z = 50$ and $N = 82$ remain when going toward the neutron dripline [59–61];

iii) in nuclear astrophysics, the stability of the $N = 82$ shell gap has important consequences for understanding the r-process abundance peak at $A = 130$ [62].

So far, spontaneous fission of $^{252}$Cf and $^{248}$Cm sources [63, 65, 66], as well as $\beta$-decay studies [67, 68], have been major sources of spectroscopic information in the $^{132}$Sn region. Moreover, in recent years, additional investigation became possible by employing radioactive beams produced by both ISOL and in-flight techniques [61, 68, 69].

1.2 Nuclear reactions with neutrons

The possible types of reactions induced by neutrons are strongly related to the energy of the incident neutrons and they can be used to investigate different properties of the

---

<!-- page 25 -->

nuclear structure. In this section the main characteristics of two specific reactions involving neutrons as a projectile will be presented, i.e. (i) neutron capture and (ii) neutron induced fission. These are the reaction mechanisms which are relevant for the topic of this thesis. As later discussed in Chap. 2, the nuclei of interest, in the Ca and $^{132}$Sn region, were in fact populated by these type of reactions, respectively.

1.2.1 Neutron capture reaction

Neutron capture is a nuclear reaction in which an atomic nucleus and one or more neutrons collide and merge to form a heavier system [72]. This process occurs for low energies, of the order of meV. After the neutron capture, an excited state is formed: this can be treated as a compound-nuclear state and it may be dominated by one or a few resonances. A resonant state usually corresponds to a complex configuration, where many particles and holes are involved, its lifetime is of about $10^{-15}$ s and it can decay through the energetically allowed channels (mainly $\gamma$-ray or neutron emission).

Neutron capture reactions play a relevant role in nuclear physics to determine many features of the capturing state, by $\gamma$-rays spectroscopy techniques

$$A_Z X(n, \gamma)^{A+1_Z} X^*$$

(1.1)

where $X$ indicates a nucleus of generic atomic mass unit $A$, and $X^*$ is the nucleus after the reaction, with atomic mass unit $(A+1)$, which is usually left in an excited state [72]. In heavy nuclei and very low energy incident neutrons (i.e. thermal or cold neutrons), $\gamma$ emission is the most probable decay process which populates, from the capture state, all excited states of $X$, following the selection rules for the $\gamma$ decay. Therefore, the $\gamma$ spectrum shows

i) a primary component (consisting of direct transitions from the capture state to the lower excited states)

ii) a secondary component (made of low-energy radiations between low-lying excited states), as schematically illustrated in Fig. 1.9.

It is found that the primary $\gamma$ radiation is almost not selective in populating the lower excited-states, since no strong selection rules based on nuclear structure will forbid primary decays to any particular excited state: the decay from the capture state occurs in an almost pure statistical way. This is in contrast to $\alpha$- or $\beta$-decay, in which the emission process is governed by stronger selection rules which may forbid decays to states that one could expect to be populated, considering angular momentum couplings only. Neutron capture can therefore serve to achieve an almost complete spectroscopy of excited

---

<!-- page 26 -->

states, as nicely demonstrated by the neutron capture reactions on Ca targets, discussed in Chap. 4 of this thesis, and in Ref. [73]. The latter reports an extended, complete $\gamma$ spectroscopy study of $^{210}$Bi, performed by neutron capture during the EXILL experimental campaign.

To conclude, it is important to remark that $(n,\gamma)$ reactions are relevant in many other fields, as for example Astrophysics (here neutron capture plays an important role in the cosmic nucleus-synthesis of heavy elements); Health Physics (as for example neutron capture for cancer therapy); Applications (e.g., neutron activation analysis techniques for environment pollution research, archaeology, production of radioisotope, ...).

1.2.2 Neutron induced fission

Few years after the discovery of the neutron (by Chadwick in 1932), neutron capture on uranium targets was recognized, by L. Meitner and O. R. Frisch, as an effective way of producing several elements with masses distributed around $A = 95$ and $A = 140$, and a large release of energy (of the order of 200 MeV). The mechanism, called fission, is nowadays a well established phenomenon, resulting primarily from the competition between the nuclear and Coulomb forces in heavy nuclei [72]. In fact, while the total nuclear binding energy increases almost proportionally to the mass number $A$, the proton Coulomb repulsion energy increases faster, as $Z^2$, the square of the atomic number.

In a simplified picture, the emission of a heavy fission fragment can be considered as

---

<!-- page 27 -->

a process similar to $\alpha$-decay, with the heavy nucleus with an energy close to the top of the potential well, where the Coulomb barrier is penetrable, being very thin. As a consequence, fission can occur as a natural process (spontaneous fission), or it can be induced through the capture of a relatively low-energy particle (e.g. neutron) which produces excited states at high enough energy to either surmount or more easily penetrate the barrier (induced fission). In practice, the fission process becomes relevant only for heavy nuclei, like thorium and beyond.

![Schematic representation of the fission process.](image)

**Figure 1.10:** Schematic representation of the fission process.

From the theoretical point of view, the fission phenomenon can be understood on the basis of the liquid drop model, which considers only average nuclear properties and assumes the nuclei to be spherical. This is a rather crude approximation, since massive nuclei most often do not have a spherical equilibrium shape. In addition, surface waves can be created by an external perturbation, as for example an incident neutron, leading to a change in the shape of a liquid drop, which becomes elongated. At this point, if the resulting deformation is sufficiently large, Coulomb repulsion may produce a two-lobe structure, causing the fission of the initial nuclear drop into two droplets, as shown in the cartoon sequence reported in Fig. 1.10.

A quantitative description of the fission process can also be obtained by the liquid drop model, starting from the semi-empirical formula for the binding energy

$$B.E. = a_V A - a_S A^{\frac{2}{3}} - a_C \frac{Z^2}{A^{\frac{1}{3}}} - a_A \frac{(N-Z)^2}{A} \pm \delta A^{-\frac{1}{2}}$$  \hspace{1cm} (1.2)

with

$$a_V = 1.56 \quad a_S = 17.23 \quad a_C = 0.70 \quad a_A = 23.29 \quad \delta = 12.0$$

where the previous parameters (expressed in MeV) represents the Volume, Surface, Coulomb, Asymmetry and Pairing contribution, respectively. To describe the fission process,

---

<!-- page 28 -->

the spherical liquid drop of radius $R$ must be slightly deformed, as a consequence of an external perturbation, to an ellipsoid of the same volume, with semi-major and semi-minor axes $a$ and $b$, respectively. Therefore, the previous five terms are written in terms of a small deformation parameter epsilon, as follows

$$a = R(1 + \epsilon)$$
$$b = \frac{R}{(1 + \epsilon)^{1/2}}$$

(1.3)

since the volume for the sphere and the ellipsoid is identical, the volume energy will remain the same for both the spherical and the deformed liquid drops, while the surface energy and the Coulomb energy will be different. More precisely, the deformation increases the surface energy but decreases the Coulomb term

$$a_S A^{2/3} \rightarrow a_S A^{2/3} \left(1 + \frac{2}{5} \epsilon^2\right)$$
$$a_C \frac{Z^2}{A^{1/3}} \rightarrow a_C \frac{Z^2}{A^{1/3}} \left(1 - \frac{1}{5} \epsilon^2\right)$$

(1.4)

The competition between the previous two terms determines the stability of the droplet against fission, as can be seen if one considers the total change in binding energy, due to deformation:

$$\Delta = \text{B.E. (ellipsoid)} - \text{B.E. (sphere)}$$
$$= \left(-\frac{2}{5} a_2 A^{2/3} + \frac{1}{5} a_3 \frac{Z^2}{A^{1/3}}\right)$$

(1.6)

If the total change in energy difference $\Delta$ is positive, the system gains energy through the stretching, becomes unstable and undergoes fission. Therefore, the condition for spontaneous fission becomes

$$\frac{1}{5} a_C \frac{Z^2}{A^{1/3}} > \frac{2}{5} a_S A^{2/3} \quad \text{or} \quad \frac{Z^2}{A} > 47$$

(1.7)

where the experimental values of $a_S \approx 17.23$ and $a_C \approx 0.70$ were used.

The parameter $Z^2/A$ is called “fissionability parameter” and can be considered as a rough indicator of the stability of a nucleus to spontaneous fission. A more precise estimate should take into account, first of all, the quantum mechanical barrier penetration, which permits spontaneous fission even when the deformation energy is negative. In addition, permanent equilibrium shape must be considered, as it happens for nuclei in the uranium region.

The way a nucleus fissions into two fragments is not uniquely defined. As a consequence, there is a mass distribution of the fission products, symmetric about the center and varying with the fissile isotope. Figure 1.11(a) shows fission fragment mass distribution in the case of thermal-neutron induced fission of $^{234}$U, $^{236}$U and $^{240}$Pu, and of $^{252}$Cf spontaneous fission. It is seen that for every heavy fragment, there is a corresponding light fragment. However, fission into equal or nearly equal fragments is less probable by a factor of about 600, with respect to the maximum yield of fragments centered around mass $A_1 = 95$ and $A_2 = 140$. This is a clear influence of the shell structure of the nucleus, which becomes less important at large excitation energy, as a consequence of the high density of states through which the fission process goes. As shown in Fig. 1.11(b), asymmetric fission tends to the symmetric case when the projectile energy increases. Figure 1.11(c) shows the Z versus N, two-dimensional mass distributions of fission fragments produced by spontaneous fission of $^{252}$Cf (top) and cold-neutron induced fission of $^{235}$U (bottom). The $^{235}$U case corresponds to one of the fission reactions studied in this work (see Chap. 5) and clearly shows a more favourable population of light mass fragments, north-west of $^{78}$Ni.

Fission induced by neutrons in actinide nuclei is the best studied fission reaction. This is due to the importance in energy production and also to the extremely large cross sections in the case of nuclei like $^{235}$U, following thermal neutron capture.

In the reaction $^{235}$U($n_{\text{th}}, f$) with thermal neutrons of energy $E_n = 25$ meV the fission cross section $\sigma_f = 586$ b is huge and about 340 times larger than the geometrical cross section of $^{236}$U. This is a consequence of the large neutron separation energy $S_n = 6.5$ MeV in $^{236}$U, which exceeds the fission barrier $B_f = 5.6$ MeV. Therefore, for thermal neutrons the excitation energy $E^*$ of the compound is $E^* = S_n > B_f$ and the isotope $^{235}$U is said to be "fissile".

The situation is very different in the case of $^{238}$U, the most abundant U-isotope: the neutron separation energy is $S_n = 4.8$ MeV, i.e., smaller than the barrier height $B_f = 6.3$ MeV. This implies that $^{238}$U is "non-fissile" and the incoming neutron energy required to induce fission must be $E_n > 1.5$ MeV. CHAPTER 2

The EXILL campaign

In recent years, a common and successful way to perform $\gamma$ spectroscopy of low-lying excited states of neutron-rich nuclei in the mass range $A = 85–160$ consisted in placing spontaneous fission sources of $^{252}$Cf and $^{248}$Cm (the only one available for this type of experiments) inside efficient arrays of germanium detectors, such as EUROGAM/EURO-BALL [75] or GAMMASPHERE [76]. This method allowed to study the structure of about 250 fission fragments: their decay schemes were constructed, most often for the first time, by requiring $\gamma$-ray coincidences between pairs of fission fragments, selected in a unique way. Moreover, information on angular correlations or polarization could also be obtained, giving the possibility to establish spin and parity of the excited states.

This type of studies can be further extended to neutron-rich nuclei in the regions north-east of $^{78}$Ni and beyond $^{132}$Sn, where currently nothing or little is known, by exploiting fission reactions induced by neutrons, as discussed in Chap. 1. About ten different fissile targets are, in fact, available for this purpose and of particular interest are $^{235}$U and $^{241}$Pu targets. In addition, lifetime measurements of excited levels in fission products can also be obtained, based for example on fast timing technique with lanthanum bromide scintillators or recoil distance methods. This was the main idea of the EXILL campaign, performed in 2012-2013 at Institut Laue Langevin (ILL) in Grenoble: an extended, high efficiency, $\gamma$ spectroscopy investigation of nuclei produced by neutron

---

<!-- page 34 -->

induced reactions, achieved by placing, for the first time, a large array of HPGe detectors in front of a high intensity neutron beam, such as the one delivered by the ILL reactor. In addition, neutron capture reactions on a number of stable, often rare, targets were also considered.

The experimental campaign was set up at the PF1B cold neutron beam facility, at the research reactor of ILL, for a beam time of 100 days, corresponding to two reactor cycles: the first one was devoted to spectroscopy measurements with the EXOGAM HPGe array, complemented by HPGe detectors, from which the EXILL (EXOGAM@ILL) acronym was taken; the second cycle was instead shared between with the FATIMA LaBr₃:Ce scintillator array, coupled to EXOGAM detectors, and spectroscopy with an array of HPGe detectors only. 2.1 The nuclear reactor at ILL

The High Flux Reactor (HRF) of the Max von Laue Langevin Institut (ILL) of Grenoble is one of the most important nuclear reactors devoted to fundamental research with neutrons: it produces the world’s highest continuous neutron flux for research use, using fission reactions of highly-enriched (97%) $^{235}$U [80]. The ILL reactor generates a power of 58 MW and the neutron flux at the core is $1.5 \cdot 10^{15} \text{ cm}^{-2} \text{ s}^{-1}$. Neutrons are produced, after uranium fission, with an energy of the order of MeV and “thermalize” during the collision with the atomic nuclei of the moderator ($\text{D}_2\text{O}$). At the thermal equilibrium the energy spectrum of the neutrons is a Maxwell distribution with a peak in $E_0 = 25 \text{ meV}$. This corresponds to a velocity $v_0 = 2200 \text{ m s}^{-1}$. The peak of the neutron distribution

can be moved to 5 meV (cold neutrons) and 0.2 eV (hot neutrons), respectively, by “cold” sources (30 and 25 dm$^3$) of liquid deuterium at the temperature of $T_f = 20$ K, and a “hot” source, (10 dm$^3$) of graphite at $T_c = 2400$ K. The extraction of neutrons is performed using “beam tubes” that are placed near the three sources. This allows to select several neutron beams with different energies, as shown in the diagram of Fig. 2.2. A picture of the reactor is given in Fig. 2.3.

2.2 The PF1B facility and the neutron collimation system

At ILL, up to 40 neutron guide systems transport neutrons from the high flux reactor to the experimental areas, tens of meters away from the reactor core. The most intense of these guides is the ballistic super mirror guide H113, ending at the PF1B position. The guide delivers a thermal neutron capture equivalent flux density of $2 \times 10^{10} \text{ cm}^{-2} \text{ s}^{-1}$, with an angular divergence of about 7 mrad on an exit window of $20 \times 6 \text{ cm}^2$.

The previous beam profile and divergence are not suited for $\gamma$-spectroscopy, since detectors would suffer from neutron damage and too high background. In addition, a well defined reaction point is needed for angular correlation measurements. Therefore, a highly efficient collimation system, using a sequence of apertures made from high neutron absorbing materials (i.e., $^{10}$B and enriched $^6$LiF), was developed to shape the beam five meters downstream from the end of the H113 guide. This allowed to reach a circular cross section with a diameter of about 1 cm. To avoid $\gamma$-ray background all apertures

The aperture sequence has a total length of about 4 meters, followed by a section, one meter long, of free flight path, giving access to the target chamber, in vacuum, as schematically illustrated in Fig. 2.4. The target chamber has a length of about one meter and is followed by a one meter long beam dump system.

CHAPTER 3

Experimental techniques

In this chapter, the three main analysis techniques used in this thesis work will be presented: i) the $\gamma$ coincidence method over different time windows, ii) the angular correlation method, and iii) the lifetime analysis by fast-timing. In order to evaluate the limits and prove the effectiveness of the analysis techniques, several test cases (known in literature) will be discussed.

3.1 Gamma coincidence techniques

The $\gamma$-coincidence technique allows to investigate very complex $\gamma$-spectra produced by the de-excitation of the reaction products. In particular, in thick target experiments, as in the case of this thesis work, the information about the kinematics of the reaction is lost, and the only way to identify the products, without the recoils detection, is by selecting the energy of the transitions. For this purpose, arrays based on germanium semiconductors detectors (HPGe) are better suited, owing to their superior energy resolution, about 0.2% at 1408 keV, against several % in the case of the best scintillators.

In the following, the criteria used to define $\gamma$-coincidences in the analysis of EXILL data (the event-builder algorithm) will be discussed, and a test of the effectiveness of the technique will be presented in connection with the analysis of a known case, $^{132}$Te.

3.1.1 The EXILL event builder algorithms

For most common applications, the coincidence between two or more $\gamma$-rays is defined if their detection time is within a fixed time window of about 200 ns. Coincidence histograms which are built with these conditions are defined “prompt”, and allow to investigate the states whose lifetime is shorter than 200 ns.

The “prompt” coincidence technique, based on the construction of double and triple coincidence histograms, was used to study the calcium isotopes produced by neutron capture, as discussed in Chap. 4.

In more general terms, the dimension of the coincidence window has to be chosen in connection with the lifetime of the level of interest. Therefore, if the lifetime increases, the window has to be extended. Unfortunately this procedure cannot be easily applied in the case of states with very long lifetimes (isomers) since a very wide coincidence window strongly increases the background contribution and the number of random coincidences. In order to bypass this problem another approach can be followed: if a digital, triggerless acquisition system (with a time stamp interval of the order of 10 ns) is used, different conditions on the time parameter can be requested in the off-line analysis in order to obtain different sets of double and triple coincidence histograms. In this way, two different classes of $\gamma$-ray transition can be defined: the “prompt” and the “delayed” events.

In the analysis of the fission data, discussed in Chap. 5 of this thesis, we defined

i) “prompt” $\gamma$-rays the ones in coincidence (within 200 ns) with a fission event (defined by $\gamma$-ray multiplicity equal or larger than 4, within 200 ns);

ii) “delayed” $\gamma$-rays, all $\gamma$-rays emitted within 20 $\mu$s after the fission event.

Examples of $\gamma$-ray fold distributions, as a function of time, for HPGe events collected over a 20 $\mu$s time interval are given in Fig. 3.1. Coincidence histograms were built with these “prompt” and “delayed” conditions and allowed to investigate states fed shortly after the fission and decaying within at most 20 $\mu$s after the feeding. In particular, Prompt-Prompt-Delayed (PPD), Prompt-Delayed-Delayed (PDD) and Delayed-Delayed-Delayed (DDD) matrices were used to extend the level scheme of $^{133}$Sb above the 16.6 $\mu$s long lived 21/2$^+$ isomer [71], located at 4526 keV, as discussed in Section 1.1.4. The sorting of the histograms was done with a dedicated software especially developed for the EXILL data.

From the computational point of view, an algorithm was developed which is able to label the event as Prompt/Delayed, create the coincidence and build the matrices just defined.

The event builder algorithm can be summarized in the next points:

---

1) the Prompt and Delayed window sizes are defined and $M$ is set as the minimum $\gamma$-multiplicity to have the flash fission;

2) a Delayed window is opened and it is scanned with the Prompt one looking for a $\gamma$ multiplicity larger than $M$ ($\gamma_{\text{mult}} \geq M$). Once the condition is satisfied all the events within this window are considered as “prompt” events;

3) a new Delayed window is opened next to the Prompt one and a new Prompt condition is searched. Two scenarios may occur:

   4a) if no prompt condition is found, all the events in this window are considered as “delayed” and the procedure is repeated by opening a new Delayed window;

   4b) if a new prompt condition is found, a new Prompt window is defined and all events between these two are considered as “delayed”;

In practice, the Prompt and Delayed window sizes were set equal to 200 ns and 20 µs, and the multiplicity $M$ was chosen equal to 4. Figure 3.2 presents a schematic picture of the algorithm used in the present work and explained in the previous points.

3.2.1 Effective angular correlation of cascade $\gamma$-rays

(3.1)

where $\theta$ is the angle between the two $\gamma$-ray directions, $P_k$ is the Legendre polynomial and $A_{kk}$ is the angular correlation coefficient [92, 93]. $$k_{\max} = \min(2I, L_1 + L_1', L_2 + L_2')$$

(3.2)

$$A_{kk} = A_k(L_1 L_1' I_i I) A_k(L_2 L_2' I_f I)$$

(3.3)

3.3.1 Transition probabilities and Weisskopf estimates

The electromagnetic decay processes of nuclei are described as resulting from the interaction of the nucleus with an external electromagnetic field which can be expanded in multiples containing spherical harmonics. Photons are the quantum of the radiation field, therefore the transition from the initial to the final state in the nucleus is mediated by one of the multipole terms in the expansion of the radiation field [98].

By denoting with $T_{fi}$ the transition probability per unit time, usually called “transition probability”, of $\gamma$ decay from an initial nuclear state $i$ to a final nuclear state $f$, the lifetime $\tau_\gamma$ associated to this transition (often called “partial lifetime”) is the inverse of

---

<!-- page 73 -->

the transition probability

$$\tau_{\gamma} = \frac{T_{1/2}}{\ln 2} = \frac{1}{T_{\text{if}}}$$

(3.16)

being $T_{1/2}$ the corresponding “partial half-life”. To be noted that the total lifetime $\tau$ of a level (the measured experimental quantity) is related to the partial lifetime of a particular $\gamma$ decay by the relation

$$\tau = \tau_{\gamma} \frac{I_{\gamma}}{I_{\text{tot}}}$$

(3.17)

where $I_{\gamma}$ is the intensity of the $\gamma$-ray transition with multipolarity $\lambda$ and $I_{\text{tot}}$ is the sum of the intensities of all $\gamma$ transitions depopulating the level and conversion electrons.

The electric $E$ or magnetic $M$ decay transition probability, calculated by the “golden rule” of time-dependent perturbation theory, for a $\lambda\mu$ component of the radiation field is

$$T_{fi}^{(\sigma\lambda\mu)} = \frac{2}{\epsilon_0 \hbar} \frac{\lambda + 1}{\lambda[(2\lambda + 1)!!]^2} \left( \frac{E_{\gamma}}{\hbar c} \right)^{2\lambda + 1} |\langle \xi_f J_f m_f | M_{\sigma\lambda\mu} | \xi_i J_i m_i \rangle|^2$$

(3.18)

where $E_{\gamma}$ is the energy of the transition and $M_{\sigma\lambda\mu}$ is the nuclear operator associated with the multipole radiation field $\sigma\lambda\mu$, with $\sigma = E$ or $M$. Magnetic substates $\mu$ are normally not observed individually, therefore it is possible to average over the initial substates. Next, by summing over the final substates and all $\mu$, one obtains the transition probability

$$T_{fi}^{(\sigma\lambda)} = \frac{1}{2J_i + 1} \sum_{m_i \mu m_f} T_{fi}^{(\sigma\lambda\mu)}$$

(3.19)

where the reduced transition probability is given by

$$B(\sigma\lambda; \xi_i J_i \rightarrow \xi_f J_f) \equiv \frac{1}{2J_i + 1} |\langle \xi_f J_f | M_{\sigma\lambda} | \xi_i J_i \rangle|^2$$

(3.20)

and for the inverse transition $J_f \rightarrow J_i$

$$B(E^M \lambda; I_f \rightarrow I_i) = \frac{2I_i + 1}{2I_f + 1} B(E^M \lambda; I_i \rightarrow I_f)$$

(3.21)

In the previous relations $M_{\sigma\lambda}$ represents the electric $E$ and magnetic $M$ multiple operator with multipolarity $\lambda$ and the units of measurement for the reduced transition probabilities are

$$[B(E\lambda)] = e^2 \text{fm}^{2\lambda}, \quad [B(M\lambda)] = (\mu_N/c)^2 \text{fm}^{2\lambda - 2}$$

(3.22)

Finally, using the relations

$$\frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{e^2}{\hbar c} \equiv \alpha = 1/137.04, \quad \hbar c = 197.33 \text{ MeV fm}$$

(3.23)

the transition probabilities can be put into numerical forms

$$T_{fi}^{E\lambda} = 5.498 \times 10^{22} f(\lambda) \left( \frac{E_\gamma[\text{MeV}]}{197.33} \right)^{2\lambda+1} B(E\lambda)[e^2 \text{fm}^{2\lambda}] 1/s$$

$$T_{fi}^{M\lambda} = 6.080 \times 10^{20} f(\lambda) \left( \frac{E_\gamma[\text{MeV}]}{197.33} \right)^{2\lambda+1} B(M\lambda)[(\mu_N/c)^2 \text{fm}^{2\lambda-2}] 1/s$$

$$f(\lambda) \equiv \frac{\lambda+1}{\lambda[(2\lambda+1)!!]^2}$$

(3.24)

(3.26)

3.3.2 Lifetime measurements by “fast-timing”

Experimentally, lifetime measurements can be performed employing several kind of technique. As shown in Fig. 3.12, there are both direct methods (which measure $\tau$) and indirect methods (which essentially measure the width $\Gamma$). Among others, electronic timing is the only method to access lifetimes in the range of $10^{-11} - 10^{-3}$ s, and in recent years, by using very fast scintillator detectors, even lifetimes down to the few picoseconds region could be determined. The electronic method of lifetime determination is often called the “fast-timing technique”. Particularly suited for fast timing measurements are LaBr$_3$:Ce scintillators, invented in 2001 [100, 101] and now commonly used in both research and application activities (the FATIMA scintillator array used for this thesis project and described in Chap. 2 is made of LaBr$_3$:Ce detectors). The timing properties of LaBr$_3$:Ce crystals are equivalent to very fast plastic scintillators, while the energy resolution can be as good as 2% at 1.3 MeV, implying also a significant improvement in peak to background ratio. The principle of the fast-timing technique, aiming at determining the lifetime of a nuclear state, is to measure the time difference between two coincident $\gamma$-rays, i.e., time correlated electronic signals [100]. In Fig. 3.13 it is represented the basic electronic chain used for this type of measurements: the reference start timing signal is provided by a nuclear transition feeding directly the state of interest (also nuclear decay products or a signal synchronized with the radio frequency of a pulsed beam could be used), while the second stop signal is obtained from the decay of the state of interest. In the case of no background, the “delayed” time distribution $D(t)$ is the convolution of the normalized prompt response function (PRF) of the setup $P(t)$ with an exponential decay

$$D(t) = n\lambda \int_{-\infty}^{t} P(t' - t_0) \exp^{-\lambda(t-t')} dt' \quad \text{with} \quad \lambda = 1/\tau.$$  (3.27)

i) **Slope method**

The slope method is used for lifetimes longer than the FWHM of the Prompt Response Function (PRF). In this case a precise estimate of the experimental PRF is not needed and the lifetime is obtained by fitting the straight line observed on a semi-logarithmic plot of the delayed time distribution $D(t)$, outside the region of the PRF

$$\frac{d \log D(t)}{dt} = 1/\tau \quad \text{for} \quad D(t) \gg P(t).$$

ii) **Convolution fit method**

In general, the lifetime can be extracted by fitting the entire time spectrum given by the convolution $D(t)$ function defined in Eq. (3.27). In order to calculate the convolution, the shape (centroid and FWHM) of the PRF has to be known. In general the PRF can be approximated to a Gaussian, and the convolution assumes

iv) Generalized centroid difference (GCD) method

$$\Delta C(\Delta E_\gamma)_{\text{decay}} = C^D(\Delta E_\gamma)_{\text{stop}} - C^D(\Delta E_\gamma)_{\text{start}} = \text{PRD}(\Delta E_\gamma)_{\text{decay}} + 2\tau$$  (3.32)

where $\Delta E_\gamma = E_{\text{feeder}} - E_{\text{decay}}$ is the energy difference of the two $\gamma$-rays in the cascade and the subscript “start” (“stop”) indicates that the decay transition, carrying the lifetime information, provides the start (stop) timing signal of the two detector timing system. $$\text{PRD}_{ij} = C_i^P - C_j^P$$  (3.33)

and it describes the combined $\gamma-\gamma$ time-walk characteristics of the two detector system.

As a result, the centroid difference is mirror symmetric and it is independent of the timing of both detectors.

The previous technique can be generalized to the case of a fast-timing array, such as the FATIMA array employed in the experiments discussed in this thesis [103]. If the array is made of $N$ equal fast-timing detectors, the $\text{PRD}_{ij} = C_i^P - C_j^P$ for any $ij$ detector combinations (with $i$ different from $j$) can be constructed and measured. It follows that one can define linear combinations of all array combinations of the $\text{PRD}_{ij}$, as well as linear combinations of independent centroid differences $\Delta C_{ij}$, resulting in the final expression

$$\overline{\Delta C}(E_\gamma) = \overline{\text{PRD}}(E_\gamma) + 2\tau$$  (3.34)

The previous equation represents the Generalized Centroid Difference (GCD) method and shows that the time differences between start events (i.e., the decay transition is detected by a start detector) and stop events are statistically distributed around the mean $\Delta C$ or PRD, regardless of the detector-detector combination. In this way, the analysis of a fast-timing detector array is very much simplified, as one eliminates the difficult determination of $N(N-1)$ detector timing responses. Moreover, the array centroid difference of any consecutive $\gamma-\gamma$ cascade becomes exactly equal to twice the lifetime of the intermediate nuclear state.

5.1 Spectroscopy of $^{133}$Sb

The decay scheme and the properties of $^{133}$Sb have been studied, prior to this work, by several authors in different experiments [64, 71, 110–112]. The $^{133}$Sb nucleus is, in fact, a perfect case to investigate, simultaneously,

i) single-particle, proton-type, orbitals around the $Z = 50$ and $N = 82$ double shell closure and

ii) couplings of the odd-valence proton with excitations of the $^{132}$Sn core, of various nature (see Fig. 1.7).

So far, all states having a single proton in one of the $d_{5/2}$, $h_{11/2}$, $d_{3/2}$ and $s_{1/2}$ orbitals, as the dominant configuration, have been located. In addition, a series of excitations with $J^\pi = 7/2^+$, $9/2^+$, $11/2^+$, $13/2^+$, $15/2^+$, $17/2^+$ and $21/2^+$, of which the $21/2^+$ state is isomeric with 16.6 $\mu$s half-life, are known from isomer and beta-decay studies [71]. Negative parity states with $J^\pi = 13/2^-$ and $15/2^-$ were also observed, as fed by the decay of the isomer [71].

5.2 Lifetime measurements in $^{133}$Sb

In the second part of the analysis of the fission data from the $^{235}$U and $^{241}$Pu targets, taken with the experimental setup in the $2^{\text{nd}}$ configuration (EXILL+FATIMA, as discussed in Sec. 2.4), lifetime measurements were performed.

The aim of this study was the investigation of the nature of the excited states of $^{133}$Sb, around the excitation energy of 4 MeV. In this energy region the $^{132}$Sn core exhibits a coexistence of excited states of different character, which are therefore expected to influence the structure of the one-valence proton nucleus $^{133}$Sb, as discussed in Sec. 1.1.4: while the $2^{+}$, $3^{-}$ and $4^{+}$ show a sizeable collectivity (of the order of 7 W.u.), several other states (as for example the $6^{+}$, the $4^{-}$, the $8^{+}$ and the $5^{-}$, all below 5 MeV) have 1p-1h character.

The lifetime analysis of $^{133}$Sb was done following the fast-timing procedure presented in Chap. 3 applying the two-dimensional background subtraction method (discussed in Sec. 3.3.3).

### 5.3 Theoretical interpretation

In order to interpret the experimental results obtained for $^{133}\text{Sb}$ (in particular the lifetime data), a new microscopic model has been developed with the aim of describing states with different degrees of collectivity. Indeed, as already discussed in the previous section, the large difference in the electromagnetic transition probabilities, deduced from the lifetime data, suggested a non trivial interpretation of the nature of the states involved in the decay, pointing to a coupling of the valence proton with different kind of core excitations.

In this section we will focus on the main characteristic of the newly developed approach and on the results obtained for $^{133}\text{Sb}$, in terms of energy spectrum and electromagnetic transition probabilities. A detailed description of the model, called “Hybrid Configuration Mixing” (HCM) model, will be given in Chap. 6.

The “Hybrid Configuration Mixing” model is based on a Hartree-Fock (HF) description of the particle states in the core, together with self-consistent Random Phase Approximation (RPA) calculations for the core excitations. In a very simple picture, the idea is to solve the problem in a space generated by a linear combination of single particle states $j$ and coupled states $[j' \otimes J]_j$. Thus, the results of the diagonalization will be used to calculate electromagnetic transitions for several cascades.

In order to perform the calculation for the $^{133}\text{Sb}$ nucleus, few unoccupied single particle states have been selected, namely the proton orbitals $1g_{7/2}$, $2d_{5/2}$, $2d_{3/2}$, $1h_{11/2}$ and $3s_{1/2}$. These five single particle levels have been obtained with a Hartree-Fock (HF) calculation performed with the Skyrme SkX interaction [16]. The energy spectrum of the obtained single particle states is presented in the first column of Fig. 5.10.

The $^{132}\text{Sn}$ core excitations have been obtained by means of the self-consistent RPA [22].

CHAPTER 6

The Hybrid mixing model

In this chapter the main theoretical ingredients of the Hybrid Configuration Mixing model are given [15]. The model is used to interpret the experimental results discussed in Chaps. 4 and 5.

As already anticipated in the introduction of this thesis, finding a successful microscopic description for the low-lying spectra of odd nuclei is an ambitious goal and still an open question. Despite the large knowledge and investigation of shell model calculations, Self-Consistent Mean-Field (SCMF) or Density Functional Theory (DFT), often these approaches fail in reproducing low-lying levels in odd systems. Large scale shell-model calculations are certainly appropriate for spectroscopy studies and their success in light nuclei is well documented [114]. Unfortunately, the quality of the theoretical prediction get worse when the mass increases. These calculations are still very demanding from the computational point of view, and thus have limited applicability.

All these considerations have encouraged the investigation of a complementary theoretical approach, that can be transferable among different mass regions. In this context, Particle-Vibration Coupling (PVC) models represented a good solution. This picture, introduced quite early in nuclear structure theory, both by the Copenhagen [5] or Dubna [11] schools, is based on the simple yet effective idea that particles (or quasi-particles) outside a spherical core are mainly affected by the low-lying core excitations. In this

---

<!-- page 130 -->

framework, a proper description of the low excited states in odd nuclei comes by considering the coupling between particles and core vibrations.

This kind of approach was already used in the past to describe the coupling of core vibration and single particle states, especially in the weak coupling scheme. Indeed, low-lying states of systems with a robust core, can be treated in the perturbative approximation. However, this is not always possible, since the low-lying spectra of odd nuclei may often be characterized by the simultaneous presence of states having quite different physical nature.

These considerations have motivated the formulation of a hybrid model where we included single particle states together with states that, for practical reasons, emerge from Random Phase Approximation (RPA) calculations, but can have either collective character or pure $p-h$ nature. In this respect, the aim of this model is to make a step forward toward complete spectroscopy, rather than focusing only on states with a specific character.

In the next sections the details of the model will be elucidated. In particular, in Sec. 6.1 the general aspects will be shown focusing on the calculation of the energy spectrum. Than in Sec. 6.2 the determination of the electromagnetic transition probabilities will be discussed.

6.1 Calculation of the energy spectrum

The model is based on the solution of the Hamiltonian

$$H = H_0 + V,$$

$$H_0 = \sum_{jm} \varepsilon_j a_{jm}^\dagger a_{jm} + \sum_{NJM} \hbar \omega_{NJ} \Gamma_{NJM}^\dagger \Gamma_{NJM},$$

$$V = \sum_{jmj'm'} \sum_{NJM} h(jm; j'm', NJM) a_{jm} \left[ a_{j'}^\dagger \otimes \Gamma_{NJ}^\dagger \right]_{jm},$$

where for simplicity we used $jm$ instead of the usual $n, l, j, m$. Each spin and parity $J^\pi M$ of the core excitations are labeled by an index $N$ and contracted in the simple from NJM, by dropping the parity label. $a$ and $a^\dagger$ are the usual fermion annihilation and creation operators, while the notation $\Gamma$ and $\Gamma^\dagger$ is used for boson operators. $\varepsilon$ and $\hbar \omega$ are the energies of single-particles and of RPA solutions, respectively. CHAPTER 7

Conclusions

In this thesis work, the interplay between single-particle and core excitations in one-valence particle nuclei was studied by $\gamma$-ray spectroscopy.

The data were collected during the EXILL campaign (at the reactor in Grenoble, France) between the end of 2012 and the beginning of 2013. For the first time, and for about 100 days, an efficient array based on up to 52 HPGe detectors, complemented by fast scintillators of LaBr$_3$:Ce type, has been placed in front of a highly collimated and the most intense continuous neutron source in the world (of the order of $10^8$ ns$^{-1}$ cm$^{-2}$). This allowed to perform high resolution and high statistics gamma spectroscopy of neutron induced fission products ($^{235}$U and $^{241}$Pu targets were used), besides a number of neutron capture studies on rare targets.

The analysis focused on nuclei around the shell closures of $^{40}$Ca and $^{48}$Ca ($Z = 20$), and of $^{132}$Sn ($Z = 50$) and the main results can be summarized as follows:

i) Spectroscopy of Ca isotopes
aim of the experiments was the investigation of the nuclear structure of Ca isotopes at low-spins, following neutron capture reactions on enriched “$^{46}$Ca” and “$^{48}$Ca” targets. Based on $\gamma$-$\gamma$ and $\gamma$-$\gamma$-$\gamma$ coincidence matrices, very complex level schemes were constructed and subsequently investigated by assigning spin, parity, and multipolarity values for a large number of states. The analysis is complete for $^{49}$Ca while it should be considered still preliminary for $^{41}$Ca, $^{45}$Ca and $^{47}$Ca.

---

<!-- page 136 -->

comparison with theoretical prediction was shown in the case $^{49}$Ca, making use of the newly developed “Hybrid Configuration Mixing model”. Calculations are also planned to be performed, in the near future, for $^{41}$Ca and $^{47}$Ca as well.

ii) Spectroscopy of $^{133}$Sb nucleus

The structure of the one-valence proton nucleus $^{133}$Sb was studied, based on the analysis of the EXILL data of neutron induced fission of $^{235}$U and $^{241}$Pu targets.

The first part of the analysis was devoted to a pure spectroscopic study of this nucleus. Taking advantage of an analysis technique based on Prompt (P) and Delayed (D) coincidences between $\gamma$-rays measured with the EXILL HPGe array, a search for new high spin yrast states has been performed. The result allowed to identity three new states above the $21/2^+$ isomer and four $\gamma$-ray transitions.

Next, a lifetime investigation of selected states was done, by the fast-timing technique with the FATIMA array. The lifetime analysis allowed to study the nature of the $13/2^+$ and $15/2^+$ excited states. Indeed, the deduced M1 transition rates between consecutive states showed an intriguing difference in the order of magnitude and brought a signature of some non-trivial change of configuration mixing in the $11/2^+$, $13/2^+$ and $15/2^+$ states of $^{133}$Sb.

For the interpretation of the experimental results a new theoretical self-consistent microscopic approach was developed, named “Hybrid Configuration Mixing model”. The new approach takes into account excitations of the core nucleus of various type, from collective to less-collective character. The predictions of the model turned out to be very successful in both cases of $^{133}$Sb and $^{49}$Ca. In the $^{133}$Sb case, the model exhibits an evolution in the wave function composition with increasing spin, and it is able to reproduce the large difference in the experimental electromagnetic transitions.

The successful predictions of the hybrid model in the case of $^{49}$Ca and $^{133}$Sb encourages the use of this approach, in the near future, in a series of one-valence nucleon systems (such as $^{41,47}$Ca and $^{209}$Bi). The predictive power of the model will be tested in the case of complex excitations in medium/heavy mass systems, where standard shell model calculations are not feasible, due to the large number of configurations involved.

In conclusion, the experimental work here presented, demonstrates the importance of complete spectroscopy (based on $\gamma$-coincidence techniques, angular correlations and lifetime measurements) to pin down the nature of the excited nuclear states, in terms of single particle and collective character of the wave function composition. It also shows the importance of close connection between experimental investigations and theory development, and sets the stage for further extended detailed studies of key nuclear systems, within reach with high intensity stable and radioactive beams, currently under development.
